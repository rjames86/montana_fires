Incident Overview

Image options: [ Enlarge ] [ Full Size ]

The Southern Area Type I Incident Management Team, with Incident Commander Mike Dueitt, has assumed command of both the Sapphire Complex and the Meyers Fire at 6:00pm on September 7th.The complex includes parts of the Lolo National Forest and the Beaverhead-Deerlodge National Forest. The fires are burning in steep, rocky terrain between the Rock Creek Road and Upper Willow Creek drainage. Approximately thirty-five residences along Upper Rock Creek Road, from Stony Creek, north up to and including Wild Rose Lane. Numerous pre-evacuation notices (Level Yellow), as well as road and area closures also remain in effect. No public access is allowed within the area closure boundary. See Sapphire Complex daily updates and maps for more information.

Significant Events

Moderate, creeping, smoldering. Primary carrier of the fire are litter and duff, dead and down logs and cured grass. Many of the Douglas fir have been defoliated increasing surface fuels; however, decreasing potential for crown fire initiation. Fire has remained active above the thermal belt when an inversion is present.

Outlook

Planned Actions

Goat Creek: Continue mop-up, patrol, and suppression repair operations moving south to DP 60.

Sliderock: Monitor by air.

Little Hogback: Continue heavy mop up along Division Z and Division P. Continue assessing additional structures in Rock Creek drainage to the north. Continue mop up in steep and rugged terrain. Mitigate snag hazards where possible to facilitate mop up. Continue to provide structure protection and firefighter presence in the WUI areas near fire. Continue to monitor fire backing down Hogback Ridge toward Rock Creek. Continue structure protection in North Rock Creek (from Wildrose to Stony Creek). Continue suppression repair operation in Division N, moving north to DP-60.

All Fires: Check fire spread using air resources in areas that are inaccessible to crews. Support initial attack within the area of the TFR.

Projected Incident Activity

12 hours: Moderate to active fire behavior should continue into the early evening with a Haines index of 5 and humidity in the low teens.

24 hours: Higher humidity will arrive along with the chance for showers and thunderstorms. Extremely low fuel moistures and little change in winds or temperatures will maintain moderate to active fire behavior with isolated torching and short range spotting.

48 hours: Rain with snow at higher elevations will bring much cooler temperatures and higher humidity. Somewhat lighter winds are also forecast and only moderate fire behavior is likely.

72 hours: Rain with snow down to 6000 feet will further lower temperatures and raise humidity. Although wind speeds will rise somewhat, fire activity is anticipated at only moderate to minimal levels.

Anticipated after 72 hours: Fire behavior is anticipated to continue at minimal or moderate with cool and moist conditions through Sunday.

Remarks

The "dozer" category includes 2 dozers, 4 excavators, 1 log truck, and 2 log loaders. There are approximately 25 additional support personnel (non-209) assisting.

Current Weather

Weather Concerns

High pressure is shifting east today as a strong trough of low pressure is moving south out of British Columbia. This is allowing moisture to move over western Montana from Oregon and Idaho. The inversions were a little stronger this morning with more smoke in the valleys than yesterday. The inversions didn't break until slightly after noon today. There was frost in some of the valley locations above 5000 feet including the ICP this morning. Locations above the inversions had lows in the 50s to 60. Early afternoon temperatures were in the mid-70s to mid-80s. Humidity values ranged from 13-18%. Winds across the area were southwest to west around 10 MPH. Tepee RAWS had a gust to 25 MPH.

Moisture over Oregon and Idaho will continue to move over western Montana this afternoon into Wednesday with chances for showers and thunderstorms. Rainfall amounts of up to 0.10" are possible this evening through Wednesday morning. A much stronger trough of low pressure from British Columbia will move south over Montana late Wednesday and remain over the area into Saturday. The chances of precipitation will increase through Wednesday night becoming likely by Thursday morning. This system will become progressively colder and will bring rain and high elevations snow Thursday night through Friday night. Snow levels will start around 9000 feet Thursday night and fall through the night. The snow level could drop as low as 5500 feet by Friday morning. Steady precipitation could last from Thursday afternoon to Saturday morning. Precipitation will then taper off Saturday morning with just showers by Saturday afternoon. From Thursday to Saturday morning amounts could range from 0.50" in the lower elevations to 1.00" in the upper elevations with 4 to 6 inches of snow possible above 8000 feet. High temperatures could drop 40 degrees from Tuesday (low 80s) to Friday (mid 40s). The winds on Friday are expected to be from the northeast 10 to 20 MPH creating blustery and cold conditions.
