Burnout Operation Begins on South Side of Rice Ridge Fire

Incident: Rice Ridge Fire Wildfire
Released: 25 min. ago

Burnout operations started at 2 pm this afternoon on the south side of the Rice Ridge fire on the ridge above Forest Road 477 west of Monture Cabin. The starting area for this firing operation is about 2.5 miles upslope and north from the 477 road on ridge tops in the area between Little Stanley Creek/ Black Canyon Creek on the west and Cave Creek/Dunham Creek on the east. This area is too steep and inaccessible for firefighters to slow or stop the fire where it is currently located. The purpose of the operation is to bring the main fire to the fire line.


It may take several days to complete the burnout depending on burning conditions. The area involved is between 4500 and 6000 acres in size. Smoke from the operation will be very visible along the Highway 200 corridor, at Cozy Corner and Ovando. If weather conditions become unfavorable, the operation will cease.


Here is how the process works:
A firefighter certified in operating an Aerial Ignition System mounted inside a helicopter will drop Delayed Aerial Ignition Devices (commonly referred to in the fire world as "ping pong balls”) onto the area to be burned at set intervals/spacing. Each ball contains several grams of potassium permanganate that, as it moves through the dispenser, is injected with ethylene glycol (anti-freeze), causing a thermal reaction. The balls do not explode. By the time each ball reaches the ground, the thermal reaction has produced enough heat to melt the sphere and the flame produced ignites vegetation on the ground. The fire then spreads to surrounding vegetation.

The firing operation will begin at the top of ridges. A line of balls is dropped parallel to the slope. The small fires produced by each ball eventually grow together forming a long line of fire across the slope. As this line of fire grows, it becomes a backing fire which moves down the slope at a low intensity. A second line of balls is dropped downslope from the first line. If the second line of fire burns upslope, it quickly runs into the black line created by the previous line of fire above it before it can develop any intensity. By continuing stringing lines of fire down and parallel to the slope, the entire fire area will eventually back down close enough to the fire line that crews along the 477 road can do hand ignition to complete the burnout operation. If any of the burning lines start getting a little too intense, helicopters can drop water from buckets to slow the fire intensity. This combination of fire and water will keep the fire burning at low intensity as it backs down the mountain, keeping the fire from growing in intensity.

The purpose of keeping the fire moving at a reduced intensity is to make sure that it can't build enough heat to create a crown fire that would quickly run back upslope and destroy trees. This allows firefighters to manage the fire under a controlled condition to get the main fire to the fire line where they can have better access to control it.

Unless we bring the main fire to the fire line on our terms, the fire will eventually find a way to move down the mountain on its own terms which could have serious consequences, especially if the fire were wind driven and had a high potential to escape control lines.

As of 5 pm today, all operations on the burnout are going according to plan.
