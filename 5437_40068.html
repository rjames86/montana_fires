Liberty Fire Update

Tuesday, September 5, 2017, 8:30 am

Northern Rockies Incident Management Team

Greg Poncin, Incident Commander


Fire Information: 406-272-4354, 8 am – 8 pm

Email: LibertyFire2017@gmail.com

Websites: inciweb.nwcg.gov/incident/5437/#

Facebook: https://www.facebook.com/lolonationalforest/

Location: Seventeen miles southeast of Arlee, Montana in the South Fork Primitive Area

Evacuations: Currently, there are no evacuation warnings or orders

Yesterday’s Accomplishments: East winds were experienced Monday, but did not disperse smoke from area fires that has settled and created a strong inversion. The inversion aided firefighters as fire activity moderated, although active around and within the perimeter. Active fire behavior on Sunday caused spotting across containment lines and yesterday crews worked to line and contain those areas. Available and incoming resources evaluated the fire perimeter and assessed areas where additional lines could be implemented and existing lines could be strengthened. Aerial resources were unable to fly due to the inversion.

Today’s Planned Actions: Today is a transition day on the fire from Taiga Rohrer’s Great Basin Incident Management Team to Greg Poncin’s Northern Rockies Incident Management Team so firefighters and managers will take extra time to familiarize themselves with the fire. Firefighters will continue to secure fire line on the south and southeast edge of the fire and burnout unburned islands of fuel to Belmont Road. Firefighters will also burnout in the Boles Creek Area to secure work already completed in the northeast edge of the fire by the Primitive Forest and the 2007 Jocko Lakes fire. This will provide for firefighter safety as work is expected to begin along Finley Creek and Placid Creek roads.

Weather and Fire Behavior: East winds will continue today with high pressure building over the fire causing smoke to once again dominate the landscape and limit air operations. If smoke does lift later in the day it will filter back in quickly. A warming and drying trend is expected to continue through Thursday. Fire behavior is expected to be less active in the morning and pick up in the afternoon as smoke lifts from the fire perimeter. Torching, spotting and short crown runs remain possible as fuel moisture remains extremely dry. Continued creeping is also expected in heavy fuels.

Air Operations: Aircraft assigned to the fire are available for this incident and for initial attack. A temporary flight restriction is in effect over the fire area. Flying drones over the fire is prohibited. If drones are detected, air operations will stop.

Closures: An area closure is in place for public health and safety due to wildfire. The closure area begins on the Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes (CSKT) reservation Boundary line and continues east following the CSKT Boundary along the Middle Fork of Jocko River and Jocko Lake turning southwest; then heading East and southeast crossing Second Creek, Grouse Creek, Buck Creek and Finley Creek; then south to Boles Creek. Continuing due east to Vaughn Creek, then due south crossing North Fork Blanchard Creek, Cold Brook and Blanchard Creek; continuing west crossing Belmont Creek and Gold Creek then turning northwest crossing Spring Creek, West Fork Gold Creek, next to and including Boulder Lake, Wapiti Lake and Crazy Fish Lake; heading north on a straight line until it intersects with the point of beginning.

All trails and roads that are within, and those that make up the boundaries to this area closure, are closed due to the Liberty fire. The South Fork Primitive area of Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes is closed.

Agencies with Jurisdiction: Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes, Montana DNRC, and Lolo National Forest

Cooperating Agencies: Bureau of Land Management, Bureau of Indian Affairs, Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes, CSKT Department of Emergency Services, Lake County Office of Emergency Management, Lake County Office of Emergency Services, Missoula County and Seeley Lake Fire, Montana DNRC, and The Nature Conservancy
